Capstone

Capstone Presentation

The closing project of the internship: every department rotation, synthesized by the full cohort into one final presentation for Evisions employees and leadership.

01Description

The internship was built around one central goal: giving interns a complete, ground-level understanding of how a real B2B (business-to-business) software company operates, not from one department, but from every function that touches the customer, the product, and the business itself. The capstone closes that arc. It required every intern to synthesize everything learned across all seven department rotations into a single, cohesive presentation of how a company takes an idea from a market opportunity all the way through to a paying, renewing customer, using the SEA dashboard as the throughline the whole way.

02How It Came Together

The capstone was never a single week of work tacked onto the end. It started in Week 1 and ran underneath every rotation that followed, with Maya Kvesic mentoring the cohort throughout the summer and a standing Friday check-in (Capstone Project Review) keeping the deck aligned with whatever had actually happened department to department that week. Eight of us built it together: Eadlin Morrissey, Delaney Niebo, Sourish Gadhi, Mike Wouters, Logan Foster, Athziri Vega, Jeremy Martel, and me, split across East Coast and West Coast groups, working individually, in small groups, and as a full cohort depending on what a given piece needed.

During the final week (July 20–24), the schedule shifted almost entirely toward the capstone: department open houses to fill in gaps, prep blocks with Maya, and a full run-through of the deck before we presented the finished version to Evisions employees and leadership on the program’s last Friday.

03Department Synthesis

What the cohort landed on as the key takeaway from each rotation, distilled for the final deck. Each one links back to the full week if you want the detail behind it.

1

Product & UX

Product’s biggest lesson was that evidence has to come before a feature: research proves a problem is real, and an MVP should ship only the pieces that create the most value first, not everything you can imagine. UX picked up right where that left off, treating wireframes as a way to catch structural problems before a single line of code gets written, and grounding every design choice in what the user actually needs rather than how a screen looks.

 2

Engineering & QA

Engineering turned out to be a lot more than writing code: managing and prioritizing a backlog, planning deployments, and deciding what even belongs in the product were just as central to the role. QA reinforced the same discipline from a different angle, building the habit of listing out every possible way something could break instead of trusting a single pass through the happy path.

 3

Professional Services

The clearest takeaway from Professional Services was that Argos on its own does not create value. The value comes from the collaboration around it: client relationships, dashboards customized to a specific institution’s data, and the technical problem-solving that closes the gap between a raw report and something a college actually acts on.

 4

Marketing

Marketing was a reminder that revenue follows value, not the other way around: a brand voice guide and buyer persona only matter once they turn into content, email, social, and event campaigns that make a product’s value obvious to a specific audience. Selling well looked far more like asking better questions than pitching harder.

 5

Support & DevOps

Support’s key lesson was that the technical fix is rarely the whole job: asking the right question early, communicating consistently, and protecting the line between support and paid work all matter as much as resolving the ticket. DevOps showed how development and operations now share responsibility for keeping systems running instead of working in separate silos, and how security and compliance protect real people and carry real financial weight rather than existing as a checklist. The tabletop ransomware exercise made the clearest point of the week: preparing for a worst-case scenario in advance is what makes a real incident survivable.

 6

Finance & HR

Finance’s lessons centered on cash flow, metrics, and the tradeoff between maximizing value and minimizing risk, all in service of decisions that are ultimately strategic, not just accounting. HR’s takeaway ran in a different direction: the function exists to support people and build the culture the rest of the company operates inside of, not just to manage the paperwork around hiring and benefits.

 7

Customer Success Team & Sales

CST’s core lesson was that feedback is only valuable when it is collected on purpose, analyzed honestly, and actually turned into a change, and that the best CST teams are proactive rather than reactive. Sales distilled its week into a short list that stuck: know the product, understand the customer, ask questions, use simple language, and sell yourself by building trust rather than pushing a pitch.

04Friday Courses, Tied In

The Friday bonus courses fed into the capstone too. The business track’s biggest takeaway carried straight into how I read every other department afterward: the most successful businesses create value by understanding customer needs and delivering meaningful solutions, not by building the most features. Communications closed with an equally compact lesson: conversations are remembered for how they made someone feel, not for a polished, rehearsed answer, so listening mattered more than scripting the perfect response. The full sessions — Philanthropy, Communications, Connections, and Business — are covered in depth under Bonus Courses.

05My Role in the Final Presentation

When the cohort split up speaking roles for presentation day, I took the Support & DevOps department overview: the section covering how support performance actually gets measured, how a case escalates once it is too complex for one person to own, and how DevOps and security work underneath the rest of the product. I closed out my part of the deck by reading my own slide from the Personal Reflections section out loud to the room, the same reflection I expanded on in more detail on the Reflection page.
